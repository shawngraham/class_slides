Canada Heritage in the onset & aftermath of covid

Revenue Decline: Total revenue for not-for-profit heritage institutions dropped by approximately 5% to $2.5 billion, largely due to temporary closures affecting 87% of organisations.

Loss of Earned Income: Revenue from traditional sources like ticket sales and public programming plummeted by 68% and 80% respectively, leading 41% of all institutions to report a net loss for the year.

Reliance on Unearned Revenue: To survive, the sector became heavily dependent on public funding, which accounted for 76% of total revenue. Government emergency funds were the most critical form of support, with 64% of institutions receiving such aid

Things Looked Bleak At That Point

But it set off some new directions

Digital Content and Exhibition Design

The number of online exhibitions reached over 6,250 in 2020, nearly doubling since 2017.

Virtual Programming: Only 16% of institutions provided online public programming before 2020, but this jumped to 48% during the height of the pandemic.

Social Media and Podcasts: With 87% of institutions utilising social media and podcasts to reach audiences, there is a clear opportunity for specialist content creators and digital marketing agencies tailored to the heritage sector.

Technical Services and Digitisation

Digitisation and Data Management: Approximately 36% of heritage institutions worked on digitising their collections, and 35% focused on improving the quality and accuracy of existing collection records.

Digital Preservation: About 24% of institutions focused on enhancing digital preservation activities, such as maintaining hard drives and long-term digital storage. Businesses offering specialised archival technology and data cleaning services could find significant opportunities here.

Virtual Revenue Streams: Only 18% of organisations attempted to monetise virtual offerings in 2020, suggesting a vast untapped market for consultancy in this area.

Membership Innovation:

There is an opening for developing "virtual-benefit" membership models—including exclusive podcasts or virtual tours—which currently only 1% of institutions have implemented.

The Right Structure For The Job Redux

Structure Primary Objective Best Appropriate For...? Key Financial Implication
Sole Proprietorship Personal income & control Solo consultants, independent researchers, artisans monitoring a hobby-based business Owner is personally liable for all business debts
Partnership Shared risk/reward Small teams of historians/researchers pooling resources Profits and liabilities are shared amongst partners
For-Profit Corporation Wealth generating & growth (does it have to be about growth?) Scalable tech startups (e.g., dh software? See Greenhouse Studios for counter), hospitality Can distribute dividends to shareholders and raise capital through equity
Not-for-Profit Corporation Reinvesting in a mission Social enterprises that want to operate like a business but serve a social/cultural goal No share capital; all surplus is reinvested into the organization's mission
Registered Charity Public benefit Museums, archives, and educational foundations serving the public good Can issue tax-deductible receipts to donors but faces strict spending rules
Co-op Member benefit Artisans sharing a studio space / communities pooling funds for local social/economics Democratically owned; members vote

What does a backgrounder look like?

The goal of this document is to enable you to understand the context of this person's work so you can draw out deeper reflection on the nature of the work, the issues faced, the approaches and solutions that work, and become inspired.

This will then enable you to devise a list of questions that move us all towards a particular goal (e.g,: is history podcasting a viable field? Or, what kind of competitive advantage does training in history offer a person? Or maybe something else?)

Some questions you might try to answer in your background document

Of course, not everything necessarily applies in all cases

Organizational identity and strategic mission

what's the niche, what's the purpose?

What kinds of legal structures are used in this domain?

What's the historical lens?

How are historical habits of thoughts used professionally here?

Are we talking narrative analysis, provenance, archival management?

Risk mitigation, litigation?

How does the business identify and/or develop its audience?

How does the audience segment? (Age, education, geography, online-ed-ness, explorers, homebodies etc?

What is the unique authentic experience or 'solution' offered?

Does this business experience a 'halo' effect?

does it have an impact on surrounding businesses?

does it promote social value?

what are the impacts of its day-to-day operations?

What about labour?

what kinds of jobs are necessary for this business?

what's the ratio of high-skilled / labour intensive jobs to low-skilled jobs?

what kinds of technologies are necessary?

what's the exposure to the risks of generative AI?

what are reputational risks?

Knowing all that, you then have to create some questions to ask.

  • remember that your questions should not be a random grab-bag, but cohere to pull out the larger story: you need to know what your end goal is before you can generate good questions.
  • remember also that an interview can go places you didn't intend! This isn't about extracting-information-on-rails; it's about making space for interesting conversation.

so... given your guest, what kinds of goals might you have for the conversation?

what kinds of questions already suggest themselves to you?
